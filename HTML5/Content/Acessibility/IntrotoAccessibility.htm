Intro to Accessibility

Accessibility is being able to access content that is made for everyone.

 

Accessibility Definition: 

Accessibility is being able to interact and show everyone the complexity of your design. In the context of online, you should be able to provide information in a sensible and meaningful way to your audiences regardless of ability. Accessibility can included voice to text, subtitles, screen readers, transcripts, alt text, screen magnifier, color contrast trackers, and so much more. It should be perceivable information, operable elements, understandable in plain language and hierarchy, and functional on any device.

Accessibility is meant to help with:

  • cognitive

  • sensory

  • physical

  • overall usage of your website

 

Social Model v Medical Model:

There is the Social Model and the Medical Model of how to view disability. The Social Model focuses on the social being the bigger problem, where the environment is seen as disabling. The systems and structures are the problem, and asks the question about how we are going to solve the general social problem. The medical model looks at the individual and the individual's “problem” as the root of the repair. People do not realize that a disability is not just a health problem, but is a complex phenomenon that needs to be assessed through the social aspect – including everyone throughout the process.

 

POUR Model (Standards That Should be Followed):

  1. Perceivable: Information (and UI elements) must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

  2. Operable: Interactive elements (nav buttons, menus) must be able to be accessed and used.

  3. Understandable: Content should be able to be comprehended, learned, and remembered.

  4. Robust: Content should be available and function al on any device typically used for accessing digital content.
