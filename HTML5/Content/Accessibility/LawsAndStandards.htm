Accessibility Laws and Standards

Standards and laws that are used within the history of Accessibility.

 

Accessibility was first known for trying to get usable and accessible building and facility work in 1961. Accessibility first got wind with the Civil Rights Act of 1964 within the civil rights movement. Accesibility was not mentioned here until the protest in 1988 at Gallaudet University having a deaf president for the first time in history which paved the way for the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in 1990. This prohibited discrimination on the basis of disability.

The Rehabilitation Act of 1973 - Section 508 made it a requirement to make electronic and general technology accessible to all people with disabilities. There needs to be a standard in place to be able to access technology as the same to others. In 2017, they updated the accessibility requirements in order to keep up with the demand of technology. According to statistics more than 95% of websites are still not accessible to this day. As we have anti-discrimination laws (section 501-504) in place for people with disabilities, there is still a gap between what we need to do in order to follow these laws in place. Below are standards and models that are widely known that you could use to further the development of you platform.

 

508 Standards:

  • Useful alternative text for every picture (except decorative)

  • Transcripts and synched captions are provided for audio content

  • Contrast is used with intent

  • Data tables have to have a aligned header and not used for layout purposes

  • Have descriptive labels on elements

  • User has control over content changes

 

POUR Model (Standards That Should be Followed):

The internal standard for accessibility uses these principles about how to achieve accessibility through WCAG Gudelines. This guideline is heavily used in usability tests across the world to understand and gauge how users are interacting with their database.

  1. Perceivable: Information (and UI elements) must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

    • Presenting information according to your audience. This can mean a general audience where you use simple language to create content. People need to use the information in the way they recognize and learn. This includes having captions for videos, alt text, and contrast variables in order to have a perceivable standard.

  2. Operable: Interactive elements (nav buttons, menus) must be able to be accessed and used.

    • This includes information that can be easily usable for anyone that interacts with the platform. There needs to be usability standards, helpful alt text, and understandable headings to create a operable standard.

  3. Understandable: Content should be able to be comprehended, learned, and remembered.

    • This includes a consistent design throughout the platform and easy to understand language. It first has to have working elements that can be used by any user and then create consistency in order to be operable all around.

  4. Robust: Content should be available and functional on any device typically used for accessing digital content.

    • This includes making the platform accessible through all browsers and operating systems. Making sure that the versions of code are up to date with standards and formats within society.
