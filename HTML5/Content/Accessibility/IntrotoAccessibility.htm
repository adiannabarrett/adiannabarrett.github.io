Intro to Accessibility

Accessibility information on definitions and models that are followed in society.

 

Accessibility Definition: 

Accessibility is a person with a disability afforded equal opportunity being able to enjoy and engage with the same services as a person without a disability.

In the context of online, you should be able to provide information in a sensible and meaningful way to your audiences regardless of ability. People with disabilities should be able to understand, navigate, interact, perceive, and overall contribute to the Web in the way they want. Online accessibility should include intentional content that always displays information in any accessible way for people with disabilities.

This can be through accessibility tools. Accessibility can include voice to text, subtitles, screen readers, transcripts, alt text, screen magnifier, color contrast trackers, and so much more. It should be information that can be easily understood and should have multiple elements that are afforded to the reader.

Accessibility is meant to help with:

  • Cognitive

  • Sensory

  • Physical

  • Overall usage of your website

 

Disability Definition:

The legal definition of a disability is a restriction or limitation within physical or mental health that has a significant impact on your activities in life.

Impairment Definition:

Any partial or complete loss of functional capacity (in any way). This can be associated with hearing loss, vision loss, cognitive and learning disorders, spinal cord injuries, etc.

 

Social Model, Medical Model and Moral Model:

There is the Social Model, Medical Model, and Moral Model of how to view disability in society. As you become more aware of this you start to see the differences and similarities between the three creating stigma within the environment we have now.

The social model focuses on the environment and society being the bigger problem rather than people with disabilities. The systems and structures are the problem, and asks the question about how we are going to change the environment. It creates systemic ableism that can be seen within institutions or general places. It can also foster community in understanding that there needs to be more change within places to be more inclusive.

The medical model looks at a disability as an impairment in the body or body function. The model disregards people with disabilities and creates a barrier within the environment. It is seen as the individual's “problem” as the root of the repair. People do not realize that a disability is not just a health problem, but is a complex phenomenon that needs to be assessed through the social aspect – including everyone throughout the process. People with disabilities in this model need to follow the advice from “experts” to understand and fix the disability/impairment. It creates advancements in rehabilitation but creates a paternalistic view of thinking.

The moral model is the stigma and thoughts attached to having a disability or impairment. This can be seen in many movies (good or bad examples) to overly create this stigma/feelings towards someone or yourself. It can be seen as a sign of shame and blame, or can be a sign of strength creating faith within yourself/others. This is a general thought process that can be linked to either the environment, societal norms, or culture.
